Prussian blue analogue-derived Ni and Co bimetallic oxide nanoplate arrays block-built from porous and hollow nanocubes for the efficient oxygen evolution reaction†

Effective oxygen evolution reaction (OER) catalysts composed of Earth-abundant transition metals are crucial for sustainable energy conversion and storage. Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with tunable compositions are promising precursors for the fabrication of hollow and porous electrocatalysts. However, pulverous MOFs usually suffer from agglomeration during pyrolysis, greatly reducing the activity of their derived catalysts. In this work, Prussian blue analogue (PBA) arrays with hierarchical multidimensional architecture were directly grown on nickel foam (NF) using a template-oriented method. The subsequent calcination in air allowed for obtaining NixCo3–xO4 nanoplate arrays consisting of porous and hollow nanocubes. The derived bimetallic NixCo3−xO4/NF required only an overpotential of 287 mV to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2 in 1.0 M KOH solution, which is much lower than that of the monometallic NiO and the RuO2 benchmark. The 3D intersectional architecture of the NixCo3−xO4 nanoplates and the porous and hollow nanocube subunits contributed to the large specific surface area and reduced charge-transfer resistance of the NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode. Density functional theory (DFT) calculations and post-OER characterization revealed that the incorporated Co was the active sites and electrochemical active CoOOH intermediates were in situ formed during the OER. Our study provides a facile and efficient strategy for the rational design of MOF-derived materials towards effective and low-cost electrocatalysis.

Introduction

The global energy crisis has triggered extensive research on sustainable and efficient energy conversion and storage.1,2 Water splitting is considered to be a promising alternative for clean hydrogen production.3–5 However, H2 production is severely limited by the sluggish kinetics of the anodic oxygen evolution reaction (OER).6,7 By far, ruthenium and iridium oxides are recognized as the most efficient OER electrocatalysts, but their high cost and scarcity constrain their application on a large scale.8,9 Accordingly, materials based on transition metal oxides have attracted growing attention because of their natural abundance and catalytic activity.10,11 Among them, the first-row transition metal (Co, Fe, Ni)-based oxides with theoretically high activity are regarded as potential substitutes for noble metal-based catalysts.6,9,12,13 Bimetallic oxides with the incorporation of an additional metal element are more beneficial for electrochemical water oxidation than their monometallic counterparts, because they are endowed with enhanced electronic structure and optimized adsorbate energy.3,14–20 Therefore, the development of non-noble transition metal-based catalysts with diverse compositions is of great significance for clean and efficient electrochemical water oxidation currently.21

Metal–organic frameworks (MOFs) with highly ordered porous structure, intrinsic open framework, and tunable compositions are believed to be promising precursors for the fabrication of electrocatalysts.22–29 Prussian blue analogues (PBAs) with the formula of MII3[MIII(CN)6]2·nH2O (M = Fe, Co, Ni, Mn, Cu, etc.) contain rich and diverse metal ions, representing an important class of MOFs.29,30 Considerable efforts have been devoted to fabricating hollow and porous materials derived from MOFs.29,31–34 For example, ZnO/ZnFe2O4/C octahedra with hollow interiors and porous walls were prepared by employing a MOF as both the precursor and the template.35 MOF-derived hollow and porous materials boost excellent activity towards the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) and the OER,21,25,36,37 originating from large specific surface area, ample electrochemically active sites, and enlarged contact area between the electrode and electrolyte that benefit mass diffusion and reactions.

At present, most MOFs reported are pulverous materials prepared from homogeneous solution reactions between soluble metal ions and organic ingredients.21,38,39 For instance, PBA nanocages built from pyramid-like walls were fabricated using a precipitation method followed by a chemical etching method. The Ni–Co mixed oxides derived from PBA nanocages exhibited enhanced OER activity with an overpotential of 380 mV at the current density of 10 mA cm−2.37 Nevertheless, the performance of powder-form catalysts are not as good as the performance of those growing directly on conductive substrates because pulverous materials have to be glued to the substrate with the help of costly conductive polymeric binders, resulting in the decrease of catalytic active sites and the stability of the electrode under vigorous gas evolution.25,40 In addition, the pyrolysis of MOFs would inevitably cause agglomeration of particles that strongly lower the activity of the derived catalysts.40 Recently, an alternative procedure to synthesize MOFs directly on conductive substrates has been developed using solid matters (e.g. metals, metal oxides, metal hydroxides, and metal carbonates) as both growth templates and metal sources.26,39,41 It is reported that three-dimensional (3D) Ni–Fe PBA arrays of the ordered spatial organization were prepared by adopting nickel hydroxide arrays as precursors. Carbon-wrapped Fe2P and Ni2P arrays obtained by further phosphidation only needed an overpotential of 70 and 121 mV to deliver a current density of 10 mA cm−2 under acidic and alkaline conditions respectively for the HER.25 On the basis of these premises, it is promising to develop a highly efficient and stable MOF-derived electrocatalyst with well-aligned arrays of porous and hollow structures growing directly on the conductive substrate.

In this work, we propose a facile template-oriented strategy of synthesizing Ni–Co PBA nanoarrays to obtain hierarchical multidimensional NixCo3−xO4 nanoarrays block-built from porous and hollow nanocubes on nickel foam (NF). Regular nickel hydroxide nanoplate arrays were first fabricated on NF as templates and metal reservoirs. Then, the nickel hydroxide nanoplate was treated with the K3[CoIII(CN)6] solution to prepare the Ni–Co PBA nanoplate arrays built from closely packing nanocubes. Subsequent calcination in air led to the obtainment of NixCo3−xO4 arrays consisting of porous and hollow nanocube subunits. The produced NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode needed only an overpotential of 287 mV to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2 in 1.0 M KOH solution. Moreover, the derived electrode exhibited excellent stability in an alkaline medium as no obvious increment of overpotential was observed after a chronopotentiometry test for 24 h. The enhanced OER performance of the NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode can be ascribed to the following aspects. (1) The 3D intersecting architecture of the nanoplate arrays enlarged the specific surface area and provided abundant active sites. (2) The porous and hollow structure of each nanocube building block enhanced the surface permeability and facilitated the release of gas bubbles on the surface. (3) The direct growth of the catalyst on the conductive substrate reduced the charge-transfer resistance. (4) The Co sites were proven to be active for the OER and the electrochemically active CoOOH intermediates were in situ formed during the electrocatalysis process.42

Experimental

Materials synthesis

Synthesis of Ni(OH)2/NF. The preparation of Ni(OH)2/NF was based on the reported procedures with slight modifications.25 NF was cleaned in HCl aqueous solution, ethanol and deionized water for 10 min, respectively. At first, 1.5 mmol Ni(NO3)2·6H2O, 7.5 mmol urea and 3 mmol NH4F were dissolved in 30 mL deionized water under vigorous stirring for 10 min. Subsequently, the as-obtained clear solution was transferred into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave and a piece of cleaned NF (2 cm × 5 cm) was dipped into the solution. Then, the autoclave was sealed and maintained at 120 °C for 10 h. After cooling to room temperature naturally, the obtained Ni(OH)2/NF was washed with deionized water and ethanol and dried at 60 °C.
Synthesis of Ni–Co PBA/NF. 0.22 mmol K3[CoIII(CN)6] was dissolved in 30 mL deionized water. Then, the solution was transferred into a 50 mL Teflon-lined stainless autoclave and the as-prepared Ni(OH)2/NF was dipped into the solution. The autoclave was sealed and maintained at 120 °C for 30 h. After cooling to room temperature naturally, the obtained Ni–Co PBA/NF was washed with deionized water and ethanol and dried at 60 °C.
Synthesis of NixCo3−xO4/NF. The as-prepared Ni–Co PBA/NF was annealed at 350 °C for 2 h with a heating rate of 2 °C min−1 in air. NixCo3−xO4/NF was obtained after naturally cooling to room temperature.
Synthesis of NiO/NF. The as-prepared Ni(OH)2/NF was straight annealed at 350 °C for 2 h with a heating rate of 2 °C min−1 in air. The NiO/NF was obtained after naturally cooling to room temperature.
Synthesis of pulverous Ni–Co PBA (Ni–Co PBA P). The preparation of Ni–Co PBA P was based on the reported procedures with slight modifications.37 Solution A was prepared by dissolving 0.6 mmol Ni(NO3)2·6H2O and 0.9 mmol sodium citrate in 20 mL deionized water. Solution B was prepared by dissolving 0.4 mmol K3[CoIII(CN)6] in 20 mL deionized water. The two solutions were then mixed under vigorous stirring for 10 min. The obtained solution was aged for 24 h at room temperature. The precipitate was collected using centrifugation, washed with deionized water and ethanol, and dried at 60 °C.
Synthesis of pulverous NixCo3−xO4 (NixCo3−xO4 P). The as-prepared Ni–Co PBA P was annealed at 350 °C for 2 h with a heating rate of 2 °C min−1 in air. NixCo3−xO4 P was obtained after naturally cooling to room temperature.

Characterization

The morphology was explored by field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FESEM, FEI Nova NanoSEM 230) and transmission electron microscopy (TEM, JEM 200CX TEM), both of which are equipped with energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDX) analyzer. The crystal structure was measured by X-ray diffraction (XRD, Rigaku Ultima III, Cu Kα radiation). The chemical states were detected by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS, Thermo Fisher Scientific, Kα). The specific surface area and the pore size distribution were investigated by nitrogen adsorption–desorption at 77 K using automatic volumetric sorption apparatus (Micromeritics ASAP 2020) and counted by the Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) method.

Electrochemical measurements

Electrochemical measurements were conducted using a CHI660E electrochemical workstation in a standard three-electrode system in 1.0 M KOH solution. A saturated calomel electrode (SCE) was adopted as the reference electrode and a graphite rod as the counterelectrode. The NixCo3−xO4/NF, NiO/NF, Ni–Co PBA/NF, Ni(OH)2/NF and bare NF self-supported electrodes were employed directly as the working electrodes. The working electrodes of commercial RuO2 and NixCo3−xO4 powders were prepared by spreading the well-dispersed catalyst ink on NF. All potentials were calibrated to the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) based on the equation: E (RHE) = E (SCE) + 0.0591 pH + 0.244. The iR compensation was applied to all the electrochemical data. Before recording the electrochemical data, all the working electrodes were activated to a stable state by cyclic voltammetry (CV) at a scan rate of 100 mV s−1. The linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) data were obtained at a scan rate of 5 mV s−1. The double-layer capacitance (Cdl) value was determined from the CV curves measured at different scan rates from 1.05 to 1.15 V (vs. RHE). Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) was performed in the frequency range between 0.01 Hz and 100 kHz at an overpotential of 420 mV.

Results and discussion

Compositional and structural characterization

As depicted in Fig. 1a, interpenetrating nickel hydroxide nanoplate arrays with smooth surfaces (Fig. 2a) were first grown on nickel foam (Ni(OH)2/NF) using a hydrothermal procedure. The Ni(OH)2/NF was then immersed in an aqueous solution containing 0.22 mmol K3[CoIII(CN)6] at 120 °C. After reacting for 30 h, the smooth surfaces of the nanoplates became rough, and a hierarchical architecture was formed, with nanocubes densely packed into two-dimensional (2D) plates (Fig. 2b). The light green color of Ni(OH)2/NF subsequently changed into light blue after the reaction (Fig. 1b). The X-ray diffraction (XRD) peaks for pristine nickel hydroxide were ascribed to the Ni(OH)2·0.75H2O phase (JCPDS no. 38-0715) and the Ni(OH)2 phase (JCPDS no. 14-0117) (Fig. 2c). After reacting with K3[CoIII(CN)6], the diffraction peaks for the obtained sample matched well with Ni3[Co(CN)6]·12H2O (Ni–Co PBA) (JCPDS no. 89-3738).36 Note that no obvious peaks for neither Ni(OH)2·0.75H2O nor Ni(OH)2 were observed compared with pristine nickel hydroxide, indicating a complete transformation of Ni(OH)2/NF into Ni–Co PBA/NF.

Fig. 2 FESEM images of (a) Ni(OH)2/NF and (b) Ni–Co PBA/NF and (c) their corresponding XRD patterns (peaks at 44°, 52° and 76° ascribed to the Ni substrate).

In this template-oriented process, known as the pseudomorphic replication method, the daughter Ni–Co PBA arrays preserved the shape and dimension of the parent arrays of nickel hydroxide well.26 With immersion in the K3[CoIII(CN)6] solution at increased temperature, the nickel hydroxide precursor slowly dissolved and released nickel ions at the solid/liquid interface.26,42 The organic ligands of [Co(CN)6]3− then immediately coordinated with the Ni ions and in situ crystallized into Ni–Co PBA.26 With longer reaction time, nickel hydroxide was totally depleted and the PBA cubes grew larger until they closely packed into ordered 2D plate-like arrays and formed a hierarchical multidimensional architecture.25,43 The NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode was finally obtained by calcination of the Ni–Co PBA/NF in air at 350 °C for 2 h. The color of the sample turned black after thermal treatment (Fig. 1b). For comparison, analogous NiO/NF was prepared by directly calcinating the primitive Ni(OH)2/NF in air for the same temperature and time.

The field-emission scanning electron microscopy (FE-SEM) images of the NixCo3−xO4/NF with different magnifications demonstrated that the hierarchical structure remained unchanged with the nanocube of 100–300 nm packed into interweaved plates (Fig. 3a–c). The oblique view of a single NixCo3−xO4 nanocube gave a 3D presentation of the hollow structure with translucent walls and void interior (Fig. 3d). The XRD pattern failed to show the precise crystal phase of the NixCo3−xO4/NF because the poor crystallinity made its diffraction peaks to be covered by the strong peaks of the nickel substrate (Fig. S1a†). A similar phenomenon was observed for the NiO/NF owing to a similar reason (Fig. S1b†).


Fig. 3 (a–c) FESEM images of NixCo3−xO4/NF. (d) Enlarged view of the FESEM image of a single nanocube in NixCo3−xO4/NF.

Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) measurements were carried out to further explore the detailed structural properties of the NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode. Fig. S2† shows a small sheet-like specimen constructed from several nanocubes. Fig. 4a illustrates a single NixCo3−xO4 nanocube separated from the others by sonication, the faces of which shrank into concaves after thermal treatment. The darker outer edge and the brighter inner parts showed strong contrast in the cube, which gave conclusive evidence for a hollow structure of an individual nanocube.33 Each single nanocube was composed of smaller nanoparticles and exhibited a porous structure. Fig. 4b presents the schematic illustration of the hollow and porous structure of a single NixCo3−xO4 nanocube. For the NiO/NF reference, the plate-like morphology of pristine nickel hydroxide was well preserved after thermal treatment (Fig. S3 and S4a†). The high-resolution transmission electron microscopy (HRTEM) image of NixCo3−xO4/NF in Fig. 4c revealed apparent lattice fringes with interplanar spacings of 0.245 nm and 0.234 nm corresponding to the (311) and (222) crystal planes of the spinel Ni–Co oxide phase (NiCo2O4, JCPDS no. 20-0781), respectively. For NiO/NF, the HRTEM image showed a lattice fringe of 0.241 nm corresponding to the (111) plane of NiO (JCPDS no. 47-1049) (Fig. S4b†). EDX spectrum gave an Ni/Co atomic ratio of ∼2 in NixCo3−xO4. The high-angle annular dark field (HAADF) image and the corresponding STEM-EDX elemental mapping of a single nanocube in the NixCo3−xO4/NF show the homogeneous distribution of Ni, Co, and O elements within the nanocube (Fig. 4d). Notably, the obviously dense number of dots at the edge more than that of the inner parts of Ni, Co and O indicates more elements at the edge, further confirming the hollow structure of each single nanocube. The hollow structure not only increased the contact area between the catalyst and the electrolyte by exposing both the outer and inner surfaces of the nanocubes, but also boosted the release of O2 bubbles by shortening the diffusion length of the evolved gas.44 The SEM-EDX elemental mapping for the whole plate also showed the overall uniform distribution of the three elements on a large scale (Fig. S5a†).


Fig. 4 (a) TEM image and (b) schematic illustration of a single NixCo3−xO4 nanocube. (c) HRTEM image, and (d) HAADF image and corresponding STEM-EDX elemental mapping of NixCo3−xO4.

The porosity of NixCo3−xO4/NF was quantified by measuring N2 adsorption–desorption isotherms and pore size distribution (Fig. S6†). The isotherm profile exhibited a hysteresis loop at a relative pressure of 0.5–1.0, indicating the existence of mesopores, in good agreement with the results observed from the TEM images.45,46 The Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) specific surface area was calculated to be 118 m2 g−1. The Barrett–Joyner–Halenda (BJH) pore size distribution curve suggested the presence of mesopores ranging from 2 to 18 nm in size, centering at 2–4 nm. The mesoporous structure was likely formed during the process of calcination when the organic ligands of –CN decomposed into gaseous molecules of CO2 and NOx.45,47 These mesopores contribute to the enlarged specific surface area that offers more exposed active sites.46–48 The rich macropores originating from both the intersection among the nanoplates and the NF foam substrate boosted the surface permeability with easier transport channels for electrolyte ions and facilitated the continuous release of gas bubbles (Fig. S7†).20,25,28,42 In contrast to the pulverous MOFs that usually suffer from aggregation during the pyrolysis process, the present Ni–Co PBA arrays that directly grow on the NF specifically transformed into highly ordered oxide arrays of a complex hierarchical architecture after the thermal treatment and featured as a unique hollow and porous structure.40

The surface composition and chemical states of NixCo3−xO4 were probed by X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) (Fig. 5). Two typical twin spin–orbital doublets appeared in the Ni and Co high-resolution spectra together with two satellite peaks at a higher binding energy than the main peaks (Fig. 5b and c).49,50 For NixCo3−xO4, the Ni 2p3/2 peak was deconvoluted into two peaks of Ni3+ at 855.2 eV and Ni2+ at 853.8 eV (Fig. 5b).3 The calculated peak area ratio of Ni3+/Ni2+ was determined to be ∼3.5, suggesting a large amount of Ni3+ than Ni2+ on the surface of NixCo3−xO4. In contrast, only Ni2+ at 855.1 eV for NiO was observed for the Ni 2p3/2 peak without the presence of Ni3+, indicating Ni to be divalent in NiO (Fig. S8b†).15 For Co 2p3/2 in NixCo3−xO4, two deconvoluted peaks at the binding energies of 779.2 and 780.6 eV were assigned to Co3+ and Co2+, respectively, and the small peak at 772.8 eV was possibly attributed to the reduced Co0 species (Fig. 5c).3,28,40 The atomic ratio of Ni/Co was determined to be around 2 by XPS, which corresponded to the result of EDX. Three characteristic peaks acquired from the O 1s spectrum of NixCo3−xO4 as shown in Fig. 5d were denoted as O1, O2, and O3, corresponding to the metal–oxygen bonds (529.2 eV), OH− groups (530.7 eV), and surface physical adsorbed water (532.1 eV).3,51–53 The peak positions of Ni2+/Ni3+ and Co2+/Co3+ matched well with the literature for the spinel Ni–Co oxide of NiCo2O4.16,33,54,55

Electrochemical activity and stability

Electrochemical performance of the as-prepared NixCo3−xO4/NF towards the OER was evaluated in 1.0 M KOH solution in a standard three-electrode system. For comparison, the catalytic performance of NiO/NF, PBA/NF, Ni(OH)2/NF, bare NF electrodes, pulverous NixCo3−xO4 nanocubes (NixCo3−xO4 P) supported on NF (Fig. S10†) and commercial RuO2 supported on NF were also investigated under the same conditions (Fig. 6). The loading mass of the NixCo3−xO4 catalyst on the NF was determined to be 3.6 mg cm−2, and for NiO to be 3.5 mg cm−2, for Ni–Co PBA to be 7.3 mg cm−2, for Ni(OH)2 to be 4.8 mg cm−2, for pulverous NixCo3−xO4 to be 3.6 mg cm−2 and for RuO2 to be 3.6 mg cm−2. An iR compensation was applied to all initial electrochemical data. Linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves revealed that the NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode exhibited the lowest overpotential of 287 mV at a current density of 10 mA cm−2 (Fig. 6a), much lower than the other control electrodes, typical decrease by nearly 100 mV relative to NiO/NF (384 mV), and even outperformed the RuO2 benchmark (299 mV). It can be observed that the NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode afforded obviously higher current density for the OER than the Ni(OH)2/NF and PBA/NF precursors, indicating that pyrolysis was an essential procedure. At an overpotential of 450 mV, NixCo3−xO4/NF could achieve a high current density of 274 mA cm−2, over 3.6 times higher than those of RuO2 and PBA/NF, and over 8 times higher than Ni(OH)2/NF, NiO/NF and NixCo3−xO4 P. The abovementioned results confirmed that NixCo3−xO4/NF can act as an excellent OER electrocatalyst under alkaline conditions and ranks among the best compared with other reported electrocatalysts based on PBAs (Table S1†). For further insight into the OER kinetics, the Tafel plots were derived from the polarization curves (Fig. 6b). NixCo3−xO4/NF shows the smallest Tafel slope of 88 mV dec−1 among six samples measured, slightly lower than that of PBA/NF (91 mV dec−1), and much lower than those of NiO/NF (136 mV dec−1), Ni(OH)2/NF (180 mV dec−1), RuO2 (106 mV dec−1), NixCo3−xO4 P (110 mV dec−1) and bare NF (144 mV dec−1). The smaller Tafel slope was ascribed to the faster electron transport, denoting more favorable kinetics for NixCo3−xO4/NF.31 To better elucidate the origins of the enhanced OER catalytic activity of NixCo3−xO4/NF, double-layer capacitance (Cdl) and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) investigations were carried out. Generally, the calculated Cdl value based on cyclic voltammetry (CV) tests in a non-faradaic region is used to estimate the electrochemical surface area (ECSA). The detailed CV results of different scan rates were included in Fig. S9.† As shown in Fig. 6c, the NixCo3−xO4/NF had the largest Cdl value (88.55 mF cm−2) compared with its counterparts (NiO/NF: 29.10 mF cm−2, Ni(OH)2/NF: 5.27 mF cm−2, PBA/NF: 15.17 mF cm−2, RuO2: 13.29 mF cm−2, NixCo3−xO4 P: 2.52 mF cm−2 and NF: 7.07 mF cm−2), denoting the largest electrochemical surface area (ECSA) for the OER. The EIS measurement was carried out to study the charge transfer kinetics of the catalysts. As shown in the Nyquist plots (Fig. 6d), for NixCo3−xO4/NF, the smallest semi-circle among the six catalysts illustrated a reduced charge-transfer resistance (RCT) value, with faster charge-transfer ability and higher electronic conductivity, confirming quicker OER kinetics. The three-dimensional cross-hierarchical architecture of the plate-like arrays from in situ-assembled nanocubes was beneficial for charge and mass transfer, and it facilitated the release of gas bubbles evolved during the process of water oxidation.50 In addition, the mesopores offered more accessible channels for the permeation of electrolyte, and the hollow structure increased the contact area between the electrolyte and the catalysts with both internal and external surfaces available.46 The complex multidimensional architecture and the porous and hollow building blocks synergistically contributed to the large specific surface area with abundant electrocatalytically active sites for enhanced performance of the OER.

Aside from the catalytic activity, electrochemical durability is also of great significance for the development of electrocatalysts. Chronopotentiometry curve was measured at a fixed current density of 50 mA cm−2 to evaluate the stability of NixCo3−xO4/NF (Fig. 6e). Only a slight increase in the overpotential (∼10 mV) occurred after continuously testing for 24 h, which confirmed the excellent stability of the NixCo3−xO4/NF electrode in the alkaline electrolyte. The LSV curve (inset of Fig. 6e) plotted after the 24 h chronopotentiometry test showed little degradation compared with the initial one, further confirming the excellent durability of the as-prepared electrode. This was ascribed to the direct growth of the catalyst on the NF conductive substrate, which established tighter connection with the conductive substrate.42 Also, the hierarchical structure expedited the facile detachment of the produced gas bubbles to stabilize the reaction interface.56

Furthermore, after the 24 h durability test, NixCo3−xO4/NF was examined by FESEM, TEM and XPS to investigate the structural and compositional changes. The overall morphology of the arrays (Fig. S11a and S11b†) remained almost unchanged after the OER and each building block (Fig. S11c and S11d†) maintained their hollow cubic structure well. This structural stability helped to maintain the high performance of the catalyst. The high-resolution XPS results (Fig. S12†) indicated the compositional changes on the surface of the post-OER sample. No apparent variation was observed for the Ni 2p spectrum. As for Co 2p, the fitted spectrum showed an obvious increase in the Co3+ peak. Meanwhile, the O1 peak for the metal–O bond reduced and the O2 peak for the –OH bond increased, suggesting that the surface was hydroxylated after the OER, with the metal–oxygen bond partly converted into metal oxyhydroxide (M–(OH)2 or MO–OH).57,58 By combining the high-resolution spectra of both Co and O, it can be estimated that the electrochemical active CoOOH intermediates59 were in situ formed on the surface of the catalyst during the process of the OER. This result demonstrated that the Co sites were critical during electrocatalysis, which is consistent with our density functional theory (DFT) calculations and the previous report.60 However, the synergistic effect of Ni cannot be completely ruled out as the high amount of Ni3+ might help to provide a unique coordinated environment for the Co cations.

Density functional theory (DFT) calculations

To better understand the origin of the enhanced OER activity, DFT calculations were carried out to construct the free energy diagram of the OER over NixCo3−xO4 in comparison with NiO, the RuO2 benchmark and the ideal catalyst (Fig. 7d). The Ni/Co atomic ratio was experimentally determined to be around 2 by EDX and XPS, and a spinel structure was suggested by XPS and HRTEM. Thus, the possible structure of NixCo3−xO4 was built by substituting half Co atoms with Ni atoms within the inverse spinel structure of NiCo2O4. Here, NixCo3−xO4 was denoted as Ni2CoO4 (i.e. x = 2) in the calculations. According to our DFT calculations, Ni2+ occupies the tetrahedral sites, and Ni3+ and Co3+ locate at the octahedral sites in the spinel structure of Ni2CoO4. The computational details are included in the ESI.† The calculations were carried out on the most frequently exposed surfaces of (001), (100) and (110) for Ni2CoO4, NiO and RuO2, respectively.61,62 Ni2CoO4 (001) is terminated with five-coordinated Ni and Co atoms at the octahedral sites and four-coordinated Ni atoms at the tetrahedral sites. As shown in Fig. 7b and c, the adsorption of the OH*, O* and OOH* intermediates occurs on the Ru top site for RuO2 (110), and on the Ni–Ni bridge site for NiO (100). However, for Ni2CoO4 (001), the adsorption occurs on the Co top site (Fig. 7a), which is confirmed by our experimental post-OER XPS results. RuO2 and NiO have an OER overpotential of 0.65 and 0.67 V, respectively, with the formation of the OOH* intermediate being the potential-determining step (Table S2†).63 In contrast, Ni2CoO4 has a considerably reduced OER overpotential of 0.56 V with the formation of the O* intermediate being the potential-determining step. As is known, experimental overpotentials depending on the current density are proportional to the theoretical overpotential.63 Thus, the predicted trends in the OER activity are in accordance with our experimental results. Overall, the above DFT calculations demonstrate that the incorporated Co was the catalytic active sites and the bimetallic Ni2CoO4 exhibited better intrinsic OER activities than the single metal oxide NiO.

Conclusions

In summary, the Ni–Co PBA nanoarrays with well-organized structure grown on the NF were fabricated employing nickel hydroxide arrays as both templates and metal sources. The NixCo3−xO4 nanoplate with 3D cross-hierarchical architecture assembled from porous and hollow nanocubes was obtained by the calcination of MOFs in air, and it exhibited efficient OER electrocatalytic activity and high stability in alkaline electrolyte, much better than its counterparts and even outperforming the RuO2 benchmark. The exquisite multidimensional architecture and the porous and hollow structure of each building block greatly enlarged the surface area, boosted the surface permeability, and reduced the charge-transfer resistance. The DFT calculations revealed that the incorporated Co was the active sites in the OER process, during which electrocatalytically active CoOOH intermediates were in situ formed on the surface. The constructional and compositional advantages synergistically contributed to the superior activity towards the OER. Our work provides a facile and efficient strategy for the rational design of MOFs and their derived materials for the development of effective and low-cost electrocatalysts.
