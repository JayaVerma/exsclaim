Decorating WSe2 nanosheets with ultrafine Ru nanoparticles for boosting electrocatalytic hydrogen evolution in alkaline electrolytes†

Yuanmeng Zhao‡ a, Guixiang Mao‡ a, Chaozhang Huang b, Ping Cai *a, Gongzhen Cheng a and Wei Luo *a
aCollege of Chemistry and Molecular Sciences, Wuhan University, Wuhan, Hubei 430072, P.R. China. E-mail: caiping@whu.edu.cn; wluo@whu.edu.cn; Tel: +86-27-68752366
bChina Tobacco Fujian Industrial Co., Ltd., Xiamen, 361022, P. R. China

Received 16th March 2019 , Accepted 29th April 2019

First published on 30th April 2019

Searching for highly efficient and stable electrocatalysts applied for the hydrogen evolution reaction (HER) in an alkaline solution is highly desirable, but still remains a challenge due to the sluggish water dissociation kinetics. Herein, in this work, ultrafine Ru nanoparticle (NP) decorated WSe2 nanosheets have been successfully prepared by a colloidal synthetic approach. Benefiting from the interfacial synergy effect between WSe2 nanosheets and Ru NPs, the rate-determining water dissociation step for the alkaline HER and the subsequent hydrogen generation step are speeded up. As expected, the resultant Ru–WSe2 hybrid with 1.93 wt% Ru loading exhibits excellent catalytic performance for HER media, with an overpotential of 87 mV to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2, and superior long-term stability.

Introduction

The development of clean and renewable energy in order to solve the energy crisis and corresponding environmental pollution has attracted widespread attention.1 Hydrogen (H2) is regarded as a promising alternative to replace traditional fossil fuels, due to its renewable and clean nature with a zero-carbon footprint, as well as a high gravimetric energy density.2–4 Electrocatalytic water splitting is considered as a robust approach to generate hydrogen with high-purity through the cathodic hydrogen evolution reaction (HER).5–9 Efficient and stable electrocatalysts with low overpotentials and superior reaction kinetics for the HER are of critical importance in the realization of water electrolysis technologies. To date, although platinum (Pt) based materials are the state-of-the-art electrocatalysts for the HER, their practical utilization is severely limited by the high cost and scarcity.10–13 Moreover, due to the high energy barrier of the water dissociation process (Volmer step), the HER kinetics in alkaline media is about two to three orders of magnitude lower than that in acidic media.11,14–16 Therefore, the development of low-cost HER electrocatalysts with a high efficiency, especially in alkaline media, is highly desirable, but still remains a challenge.

In recent years, although tremendous efforts have been dedicated to developing layered transition metal dichalcogenide (TMD) based electrocatalysts for the HER,17–27 their catalytic performances are still far from satisfactory, probably due to the relatively low intrinsic activity and low conductivity.28–32 To further improve their HER performances, various strategies have been developed to tailor the layered TMDs for increasing the number of active sites and boosting the intrinsic activities. Among them, the interfacial engineering strategy through the incorporation of a water dissociation promoter to form heterostructured nanomaterials has been proved to be an efficient method to enhance HER activity in alkaline media.33–36 For example, the previous literature by Markovic et al. reported that the HER performance of Pt in alkaline media can be significantly increased via modification with Ni(OH)2, which is derived from the promoted process of water dissociation.33

Recently, experimental and theoretical results have demonstrated that Ru possesses maximal water dissociation capability among almost all the noble metals.15,37–39 Inspired by the previous studies, we speculate that by taking the advantage of the synergistic effect of enhanced water dissociation on Ru and rapid hydrogen adsorption on nearby TMD, the Ru/TMD heterostructure might work as an efficient promoter for the alkaline HER. Herein, as a proof-of-concept, we report the colloidal synthesis of the Ru–WSe2 hybrid with ultrafine Ru nanoparticles (NPs) anchored on WSe2 nanosheets. As expected, the obtained Ru–WSe2 exhibits excellent HER performance and durability in alkaline media, with an overpotential of 87 mV to obtain a current density of 10 mA cm−2, which is much higher than those of WSe2, and most of the reported TMD-based electrocatalysts.

Experimental

Chemicals and materials

Tungsten hexacarbonyl (W(CO)6) was obtained from Tianjin Kemiou Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd. Selenium (Se) powder, dodecylamine (DDA) and oleylamine (OAm) were purchased from Aladdin Industrial Co. Ltd. Ruthenium acetylacetonate (Ru(acac)3) was bought from Changcheng chemical Ltd. Hexane, potassium hydroxide, isopropyl alcohol and ethanol were obtained from Sinopharm Chemical Reagent Co. Ltd. Nafion solution (5 wt%) was bought from Sigma-Aldrich Co. All the chemicals were utilized without further post-processing.

Characteristic measurements

X-ray powder diffraction (XRD) was carried out on a Rigaku Miniflex600. X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed on a Thermo Fischer ESCALAB 250Xi spectrophotometer. The sizes and morphologies of catalysts were measured by utilizing a Tecnai G20 UTwin transmission electron microscope (TEM) equipped with an EDX spectrometer at a 200 kV acceleration voltage. In order to gain the element distribution and a clearer morphology of samples, mapping and high-angle annular dark-field scanning TEM (HAADF-STEM) images were recorded on a JEOL JEM-2100 electron microscope.

Synthesis of WSe2 nanosheets

Typically, 5 mL of OAm and 0.4 mmol of Se powder were added to a three-necked and round-bottomed flask under electromagnetic stirring. Then the solution was heated up to 120 °C under vacuum and maintained at 120 °C for half an hour to remove the water and oxygen. After that, the system was pumped full of nitrogen. Then the whole system was cooled to 45 °C, and 0.2 mmol of W(CO)6 was added carefully. The whole system was then heated to 280 °C and kept at this temperature for 30 minutes. After the reaction was complete, the system was naturally cooled to room temperature. The black products were washed with hexane and ethanol several times under centrifugation. The WSe2 nanosheets were acquired and dried under vacuum.

Synthesis of Ru–WSe2 nanosheets

Firstly, the stock solution containing a certain amount of Ru(acac)3 and 1 mL of OAm was prepared using ultrasound. Typically, 5 mL of OAm and 0.4 mmol of Se powder were added to a three-necked and round-bottomed flask under electromagnetic stirring. Then the solution was heated up to 120 °C under vacuum and maintained at 120 °C for half an hour to remove the water and oxygen. After that, the system was pumped full of nitrogen. Then the whole system was cooled to 45 °C, and 0.2 mmol of W(CO)6 was added carefully. The whole system was then heated to 280 °C and kept at this temperature for 30 minutes. Afterwards, using a syringe, the stock solution was injected into the reaction solution and held for another 1 hour. After the reaction was complete, the system was naturally cooled to room temperature. The black products were washed with hexane and ethanol several times under centrifugation. The Ru–WSe2 nanosheets were acquired and dried under vacuum. By changing the amounts of Ru(acac)3 (4 mg, 8 mg and 16 mg), Ru–WSe2 nanosheets with different contents of Ru were obtained.

Synthesis of Ru nanorods

Typically, 0.2 mmol of Ru(acac)3, 3 mL of DDA and 3 mL of OAm were added to a three-necked and round-bottomed flask under electromagnetic stirring. Then the solution was heated up to 120 °C under vacuum and maintained at 120 °C for half an hour to remove the water and oxygen. After that, the system was pumped full of nitrogen. The whole system was then heated to 280 °C and kept at this temperature for 1 hour. After the reaction was complete, the system was naturally cooled to room temperature. The black products were washed with hexane and ethanol several times under centrifugation. The Ru nanorods were acquired and dried under vacuum.

Catalyst preparation

The above obtained samples (10 mg) with the same mass amount of XC-72 and hexane (20 mL) were together added into a three-necked flask reactor. The solution was continuously stirred at room temperature overnight under a flow of nitrogen. Finally, the catalyst was obtained by centrifugation and dried under vacuum.

Electrochemical measurements

All electrochemical measurements were carried out at 30 °C using an electrochemical workstation (CHI 760) on a three-electrode system. The glass carbon electrode (GCE, diameter: 5 mm) loaded with various catalysts served as a working electrode. The working electrode was prepared as follows. Typically, 5 mg of the obtained-catalyst was dispersed in a 1.0 mL mixture of Nafion/isopropanol (0.1 wt% Nafion) by sonicating to form a homogeneous mixed solution. Firstly, the GCE was polished using 0.05 μm alumina powder and then washed by using ultrapure water and ethanol. Finally, 18 μL of this catalyst ink was dropped to cover the surface of the GCE (loading: 0.45 mg cm−2). The carbon rod was used as a counter electrode and the Hg/HgO electrode was regarded as a reference electrode. All electrochemical measurements were tested in 1.0 M KOH solution. Before electrochemical measurements, the real potential of the Hg/HgO electrode was calibrated versus the reversible hydrogen electrode (RHE) through a hydrogen-electrode reaction with a GCE loading with 20 wt% Pt/C as the working electrode in 1.0 M KOH under a saturated H2 atmosphere. The conversion formula is E(vs. RHE) = E(vs. Hg/HgO) + 0.059 × pH + 0.098 for the Hg/HgO electrode. The linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) was implemented utilizing a rotating disk electrode, whose rotation speed was set to 1600 rpm at a scan rate of 5 mV s−1. The EIS tests were performed in the potentiostatic state ranging from 105 to 0.01 Hz. The electrochemical double-layer capacitance (Cdl) was measured by cyclic voltammetry (CV) curves at a series of scan rates (10, 20, 30, 40 and 50 mV s−1) in Ar-saturated 1.0 M KOH. The durability and stability were evaluated by CV at a scan rate of 0.1 V for 500 and 1000 cycles, as well as chronopotentiometry measurements under a current density of 10 mA cm−2 for a continuous HER for 75000 s. The Faraday efficiencies were determined with a 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 modified carbon cloth serving as the working electrode by means of a drainage gas collection method. All of the electrochemical tests were performed under an Ar-saturated atmosphere.

Results and discussion

The Ru–WSe2 hybrids were prepared through a colloidal synthetic method. First, WSe2 nanosheets were synthesized by the reaction of W(CO)6 with Se powder in 5 mL OAm at 280 °C for 0.5 hours. Then, the stock solution prepared in advance containing Ru(acac)3 and OAm was injected into the as-prepared WSe2 solution to obtain Ru decorated WSe2. Ru–WSe2 hybrids with different Ru contents can be prepared by changing the amount of Ru(acac)3, which was further confirmed by inductively-coupled plasma atomic emission spectroscopy (ICP-AES) analysis (Table S1†). The powder X-ray diffraction (XRD) analysis presented in Fig. S1† shows that the peaks of all Ru–WSe2 hybrids and WSe2 are in accordance with the hexagonal phase of WSe2 (JCPDS no. 38-1388), while no signals of Ru can be observed, probably due to the relatively low amount of Ru or/and the ultrafine size of Ru NPs. The transmission electron microscopy (TEM) images (Fig. 1a and b) of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 display the morphology of nanosheets. Furthermore, the high-resolution TEM image in Fig. 1c further reveals that the thickness of the nanosheets is about 3–6 layers, with a 0.65 nm interlayer distance corresponding to the (002) plane of WSe2. In the enlarged portions shown in Fig. 1d and e, interplanar spacings of 0.24 and 0.21 nm fitting well with the (103) crystal facet of hexagonal WSe2 and the (002) crystal facet of hexagonal Ru can be observed, respectively, suggesting that Ru NPs are successfully decorated on the surface of WSe2 nanosheets. Moreover, the scanning-transmission electron microscopy (STEM) image (Fig. 1f), as well as the corresponding elemental mapping (Fig. 1g–i) reveal the homogeneous distribution of W, Se and Ru elements. Moreover, as shown in Fig. S2–S4,† 0.35 wt%, 4.25 wt% Ru–WSe2 hybrids, and WSe2 also exhibit a similar morphology of nanosheets. For comparison, worm-like Ru nanorods were also synthesized as shown from the XRD patterns (Fig. S5†) and TEM images (Fig. S6†).
Fig. 1 (a) and (b) TEM images of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2. (c) HRTEM image of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2. (d) and (e) The enlarged area denoted in (c) corresponds to the HRTEM images of WSe2. (f) HAADF-STEM image of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2. (g–i) The EDS mapping of Ru, W, and Se, respectively.

X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) was performed to explore the chemical states of W, Se and Ru elements of the 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2, WSe2 nanosheets and Ru nanorods. As illustrated in Fig. 2a, the surface of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 is composed of W, Se, Ru, O, and C. Both Ru–WSe2 and Ru nanorods in Fig. 2b show two main peaks assigned to Ru 3p3/2 and Ru 3p1/2, respectively.39,40 Specifically, the Ru 3p3/2 binding energy for Ru–WSe2 (461.8 eV) is positively shifted compared to that of Ru nanorods (461.4 eV). The peak of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 located at around 473 eV can be assigned to the Auger peak of Se.41 The XPS spectra for W 4f shown in Fig. 2c display four characteristic peaks for both samples which can be attributed to W 4f7/2, W 4f5/2, W 4f7/2 oxide and W 5p3/2.42,43 Furthermore, the W 4f7/2 binding energy for Ru–WSe2 (31.6 eV) is negatively shifted compared with that of WSe2 (31.8 eV). Similarly, the peaks for Se in Ru–WSe2 (Fig. 2d) are shifted to lower binding energies compared with those of WSe2. These results suggest strong electronic interactions between W, Se and Ru, which are beneficial for the enhanced catalytic performance (vide infra).

The HER performances of Ru–WSe2 hybrids and WSe2 were investigated in1.0 M KOH circumstances using a three-electrode system. The HER performances of 20 wt% commercial Pt/C and Ru nanorods were also measured for comparison. Fig. 3a displays the linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) curves of these catalysts toward the HER. As expected, after Ru decoration, all the Ru–WSe2 hybrids show dramatically enhanced HER performances over pure WSe2. Among those Ru–WSe2 hybrids, the 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 exhibits the highest performance, with an overpotential of 87 mV to achieve a current density of 10 mA cm−2, which is comparable to the commercial Pt/C (40 mV) and outperforming those of WSe2 (308 mV) and Ru nanorods (104 mV). It is worth noting that the catalytic activity of the 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 hybrid surpasses most of the reported TMD-based electrocatalysts toward the alkaline HER (Table S2†). The Tafel slopes of these samples are presented in Fig. 3b. Commercial Pt/C possesses the lowest Tafel slope of 100 mV dec−1. The 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 catalyst exhibits a Tafel slope of 116 mV dec−1, which is close to that of the 4.25 wt% Ru–WSe2 (118 mV dec−1) and much less than those of 0.35 wt% Ru–WSe2 (152 mV dec−1), Ru nanorods (167 mV dec−1) and WSe2 (184 mV dec−1), suggesting the favorable reaction kinetics of the 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 hybrid.


Fig. 3 (a) HER polarization curves of Ru–WSe2 catalysts with different contents of Ru, WSe2 nanosheets, Ru nanorods and 20% Pt/C in 1.0 M KOH. (b) The corresponding Tafel plots. (c) EIS spectra of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2, Ru and WSe2. (d) Plots of Cdl normalized by an ECSA of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2, Ru and WSe2.

Electrochemical impedance spectra (EIS) were further recorded to probe the intrinsic activities of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2, WSe2 and Ru nanorods. As illustrated in Fig. 3c and Fig. S7,† a much enhanced charge transfer process of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 compared to that of WSe2 is observed, implying a much faster reaction rate.44,45 Furthermore, we evaluated the electrochemical active surface area (ECSA) of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2, WSe2 and Ru by calculating the double-layer capacitances (Cdl).46,47 Fig. S8–S10† display the cyclic voltammetry (CV) plots of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2, Ru nanorods and pure WSe2 which were measured at different scan rates ranging from 10 mV s−1 to 50 mV s−1 in Ar-saturated 1.0 M KOH around the open circuit potential, respectively. From these scan-rate dependent CVs, the Cdl of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 (1.16 mF cm−2) in Fig. 3d is slightly lower than that of Ru (4.19 mF cm−2) and WSe2 (3.80 mF cm−2), implying that the enhanced HER activity of the Ru–WSe2 hybrid might be due to the increased intrinsic kinetics during the HER process derived from the synergistic effect between Ru and WSe2. The superb HER performance of the 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 hybrid can be mainly attributed to the rational design for boosting the water dissociation kinetics. As illustrated in Fig. 4a, the interfacial synergy effect of Ru–WSe2 heterostructures exists between WSe2 nanosheets and Ru NPs. First, the Volmer step of H2O dissociation into OH− and an intermediate Hads can be accelerated by Ru NPs. Then, the nearby WSe2, especially the W and/or Se atoms on the exposed edge can immediately absorb the Hads and further promote their recombination into H2. The interfacial cooperation could result in reduced energy barriers for both the initial H2O dissociation step and the subsequent H2 generation step, which is beneficial for the enhanced HER performance in alkaline media.


Fig. 4 (a) Schematic illustration of the synergy effect between Ru and WSe2 for an improved alkaline HER. (b) Polarization curves of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 before and after 500 and 1000 CV cycles. (c) Chronopotentiometric measurements of the HER at 10 mA cm−2 using 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 as a catalyst.

The stability of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 in 1.0 M KOH was estimated through continuous cyclic voltammogram (CV) scanning and chronopotentiometry techniques. Fig. 4b shows that almost no decline of overpotential is observed after 1000 CV cycles with continuous scanning. Moreover, as shown in Fig. 4c, an ignorable loss of overpotential at 10 mA cm−2 is observed after conducting the 75000 s test, suggesting its superior long-term stability. The characterization of 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 after the HER test indicates the well matched WSe2 phase from XRD patterns (Fig. S11†) and the well maintained morphology of nanosheets from TEM images (Fig. S12†). Furthermore, to estimate the faradaic efficiency in alkaline media, the quantity of evolved H2 gas catalyzed by 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 was tested quantitatively. As illustrated in Fig. S13,† the evolved H2 yield is in line with the theoretical value, suggesting almost 100% faradaic efficiency for 1.93 wt% Ru–WSe2 towards the alkaline HER.

Conclusions

In summary, ultrafine Ru NP decorated WSe2 nanosheets with different Ru contents were successfully synthesized by a colloidal synthetic method. Thanks to the synergistic effect between Ru NPs and WSe2 nanosheets, which is beneficial for the water dissociation and subsequent hydrogen generation steps, the resultant Ru–WSe2 hybrids exhibit outstanding HER performance and favorable kinetics in an alkaline solution, with a low overpotential of 87 mV to obtain a current density of 10 mA cm−2. Moreover, the as-synthesized Ru–WSe2 hybrid shows superior long-term stability under alkaline conditions. This work provides new ideas for rationally designed high-performance TMD-based and other non-precious metal based electrocatalysts for alkaline HER applications.
