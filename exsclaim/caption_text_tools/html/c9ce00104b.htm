Orthorhombic distortion in Au nanoparticles induced by high pressure

It is well known that the properties of metal nanoparticles strongly depend on their size. This dependence can generate unusual structures, and it enabled induction of phase transitions at lower pressure and temperature compared to the bulk materials. Bulk transition metals do not have phase transitions under ambient conditions. Bulk gold phase transitions are expected at pressures above 200 GPa. Herein, it is reported that an orthorhombic lattice distortion in single-crystal truncated-octahedral gold nanoparticles is induced by applying a high pressure below 12 GPa in a diamond anvil cell at room temperature (295 K). An asymmetrical lattice distortion of ∼3% along the lattice planes, detected through atomic-resolution electron microscopy and electron diffraction, indicated that lattice strain generated by the imposed experimental conditions led to a transition from a cubic to an orthorhombic structure. Interestingly, the mentioned lattice distortion was not observed in twinned nanoparticles subjected to the same pressure and temperature conditions. The lattice deformation took place at a much lower pressure and temperature compared to that of bulk gold, demonstrating dependency on the particle shape and structure. The experimental results reflect not only a size effect, but also a strong surface, morphological, and structural effect on the behavior of materials at the nanoscale under high-pressure conditions.

1. Introduction

The importance in engineering applications of the behavior of materials under high pressure has promoted the recent development in the research field of high-density materials, where the appearance of different nanophases is usually involved.1,2 Gold, the noblest of all metals, presents great stability, biocompatibility, and chemical inertness that have been used in countless applications,3–6 as well as a pressure standard in high-pressure experiments, owing to its low strength, moderate compressibility, and X-ray and electron scattering power.7,8 However, size and surface effects have generated unexpected structural transitions in nanoparticles at pressures and temperatures not expected in their bulk counterparts, generating unusual structures in different metal nanoparticles.9–11 Despite extensive studies, the mechanical properties of nanoparticles as a function of size are still not well understood because of inconsistent results on the size-dependence of the bulk modulus.

Bulk transition metals do not have phase transitions under ambient conditions. A phase transition of gold is expected at very high pressure. Different experimental and theoretical studies have been performed to calculate and describe the possible phase transitions in bulk gold, usually focused on face-centered cubic (FCC) to hexagonal-closed packed (HCP) structural transitions12–14 or a body-centered cubic transition.15 All of these transitions occur at pressures above at least 200 GPa.

Notwithstanding, at the nanoscale, a drastic change in the mechanical properties of different FCC metallic nanoparticles has been observed with decreasing particle size. The great advance in the determination of the effects of particle size on the mechanical properties of nanomaterials has been associated with the development of in situ diamond anvil cells (DAC), enabling the progress in high-pressure science.16–19 Through high-pressure experiments, metal nanoparticles have been proved to exhibit enhanced mechanical properties when particle size decreases. A reduction of the interatomic distances up to 10 GPa in gold nanoparticles,20 an increase of the strength in 30 nm Au nanoparticles up to 60 GPa compared to the bulk,21 a high stiffness of Ag and Au nanoparticles at a pressure up to ∼30 GPa,22 the enhancement of their bulk modulus K0 of more than 17% with respect to the bulk, and a transition in the mechanical behavior under compressive stress have been observed.23 Moreover, pseudoplasticity was observed in sub-10 nm Ag and Au nanoparticles subjected to in situ uniaxial compression24 and a non-hydrostatic high-pressure up to ∼19 GPa.25

Interestingly, it has been reported that metal nanoparticles present size-dependent phase transitions under lower pressure conditions compared to the bulk material. A reversible rhombohedral distortion of 5–10 nm-sized Ag icosahedral nanoparticles subjected to a 10 GPa hydrostatic pressure was reported by Koski et al.26 Guo et al.27 reported the transformation from a cubic to tetragonal phase in Pd nanocubes (∼10 nm) at 24.8 GPa, with no phase transition in the case of Pd nanoplates. Later, Yang et al.28 found that under pressure, high-strain areas localized at nanoparticle corners at relatively low pressure, which redistributed to the particle core when increasing the pressure. Li et al.29 reported a reversible atomic lattice shrinking at an applied quasi-static pressure up to 14.0 GPa in Au nanoparticle assemblies.

Recently, unusual crystalline structures have been reported for noble metal nanoparticles even under ambient conditions where the surface effects and internal structure play a prevailing role. A stable body-centered tetragonal (BCT) structure and a body-centered orthorhombic structure (BCO) were observed in five-fold Ag wires and Au bipyramids,30,31 generated by the internal stress associated with the requirement of closing the gap existing by the union of five FCC monocrystalline domains.32 The colloidal synthesis of gold nanoribbons with a hexagonal-4H structure was reported by Fan et al.33 Moreover, gold nanoribbons with an unusual hexagonal-4H structure have been synthesized, with a 4H-to-FCC transition induced by the effect of ligand exchange or high-pressure treatment.34 Additionally, a surface-stress phase transformation from a FCC to a non-FCC structure has also been reported in atomistic simulations of gold nanowires, controlled by their physical parameters and boundary conditions.35 The vast experimental results reflect not only a size effect, but also a strong surface, morphological, and structural effect of material properties at the nanoscale. In the last few years, this field has advanced considerably, and novel pressure-driven synthesis of noble-metal-based nanostructures has been reported.34,36–38 Strain induced in nanoparticles can lead to the enhancement not only of mechanical but also of catalytic properties,39–41 opening the possibility of a novel high-pressure methodology to synthesize new nanomaterials.

In this paper, an orthorhombic lattice distortion in colloidal synthesized truncated-octahedral (TO) gold nanoparticles was induced by the effect of applying high pressure in a DAC at room temperature (295 K). This lattice distortion generated a transition from a cubic to an orthorhombic lattice, which was found to be dependent on the particle shape and structure. The transition was determined by atomic-resolution electron microscopy and electron diffraction, which are suitable techniques for the determination of the local structure at the nanoscale. These results deliver insights into the behavior of nanoscale materials under extreme conditions, having repercussions in the design and engineering of nanomaterials with unusual phases.

2. Methods

2.1. Synthesis of Au nanoparticles

Truncated octahedral (TO) gold nanoparticles were synthesized by a standard wet-chemistry polyol method. 1.0 g of PVP (polyvinylpyrrolidone MW = 55000) was dissolved in 12.5 mL of ethylene glycol (EG), and the solution was heated at 453 K for 5 min. To the boiling solution, 1 mL of ethylene glycol solution containing 3.125 mM HAuCl4 was added. The colorless solution changed to red wine color immediately, and the reaction was continued for three min. The mixture was left to cool down to room temperature. The colloidal solution was diluted with ethanol and centrifuged at 5000 rpm for 15 min. The precipitated nanoparticles were collected and dispersed in ethanol.

2.2. High-pressure experiments

High-pressure experiments were performed at room temperature (295 K) using an Almax Plate diamond anvil cell (DAC) in which two platens attached by three screws are hand-tightened to apply the desired back-pressure on the two diamond anvils. A metallic (Inconel) gasket of 0.25 mm thickness and 5 mm diameter is placed between the anvil faces. The sample with a hydrostatic fluid is placed within a 100 μm hole drilled at the gasket center and concentrically located over the face of the bottom anvil diamond. Ruby chips are also placed within the sample medium for internal pressure calibration. A pressure up to 11.16 ± 0.2 GPa was reached, using a standard mixture of 16:3:1 methanol–ethanol–water solution as a hydrostatic transmission fluid.42 The reached pressure at the center of the cell during the experiment was calculated using Mao's equation (eqn (1)) by determining the frequency shifts in the ruby fluorescence emission line R1. The detailed methodology can be found in the work of Ojeda-Galvan.43

2.3. Characterization

Ruby fluorescence measurements were performed on a HORIBA Explora One micro-Raman spectrometer equipped with a single-pass monochromator and CCD detector at −70 °C. Excitation was provided by a solid-state laser (λ = 488 nm, 5 mW). The spectrometer was coupled to an optical microscope Olympus BX41. The laser beam was focused on the sample using an Olympus 20× objective to give a spot size of 3 μm on the sample. The data acquisition time was 1 s per scan collecting two co-added scans at a monochromator grating of 1200 grooves per mm.

The structure of the obtained nanoparticles before and after applying high pressure was analyzed through transmission electron microscopy, using a JEOL 2010F electron microscope for HR-TEM and electron diffraction, and a JEOL ARM200F aberration-corrected microscope operating in STEM-HAADF mode for atomic resolution images. High-resolution images and electron diffraction patterns of individual nanoparticles were carefully analyzed. The as-synthesized samples (i.e., particles not subjected to high-pressure) and a standard Au film deposited on carbon were used for calibration of the TEM-STEM images and the electron diffraction patterns. In this way, distortions coming from the inherent lattice strain in such small particles are minimized. An ellipse fitting method44 available as a script for the Digital Micrograph software was employed to correct the possible distortions in the diffraction patterns due to imperfect imaging lenses of the microscope. Additionally, distortions of less than 1 percent were discarded since geometrical effects, such as the specimen slightly tilted away from a high-symmetry condition, may cause small apparent changes in the position of the spots in the diffraction patterns, generating a change in the interplanar distances.45 Geometrical phase analysis (GPA) was performed within the Digital Micrograph interface using the GPA plugin developed by H. Du.46

3. Results and discussion

Gold nanoparticles were synthesized through a polyol method by using PVP as a capping agent in ethylene glycol. The produced particles presented a dominant single-crystalline structure with a TO morphology, surrounded mainly by {111} planes and small truncations exposing {002} facets. A yield of ∼70% of TO particles was obtained with the synthesis method, and the average particle size of the truncated octahedra was 22 nm ± 3 nm, Fig. 1a. Other particles such as single-twinned (triangular particles, ∼26%) and multiple twinned particles (decahedral and icosahedral structures, ∼4%) were observed in a minor proportion. The synthesized particles were subjected to a non-hydrostatic pressure of 11.16 GPa ± 0.2 GPa, using a standard mixture of ethanol/methanol/water as the transmission fluid, following the methodology described elsewhere.43 The experimental methodology is sketched in Fig. 1.
Fig. 1 Schematic presentation of the process of the high-pressure experiment in a DAC, from the colloidal solution to the experiment setup. a) SEM image of the pristine Au nanoparticles, and b) ruby fluorescence spectrum acquired at atmospheric pressure and 11.16 GPa pressure.

The structure of the obtained nanoparticles before and after applying high pressure was analyzed through transmission electron microscopy. High-resolution images and electron diffraction patterns of individual nanoparticles were analyzed. The analysis was carried out statistically on d-spacings and angles between reflections of several HR-TEM and STEM-HAADF images and experimental electron diffraction patterns acquired from multiple individual particles. The pristine particles presented the usual FCC structure of gold, and no lattice distortions were observed in the single-crystalline nanoparticles.

After being subjected to high-pressure, the compressed particles were recovered for further analysis. Shown in Fig. 2 are the HR-TEM images of isolated TO particles and their corresponding electron diffraction patterns. Two representative particles are shown oriented along the [110] and [112] axis zones, respectively. The measured planes and angles between reflections are shown. The patterns were indexed to an FCC structure. The diffraction pattern in Fig. 2b shows an asymmetrical distortion of the {111} planes, with d(−111) = 0.228 ± 0.002 nm (vector shown in blue color), and d(1–11) = 0.233 ± 0.002 nm (vector shown in yellow), as well as a change in the angle value between planes. Also, splitting of the higher-index {33h} diffraction spots along the [001] direction is noticed. The small shrinking in the (−111) spacing represents a lattice compression of ∼1.8% with respect to the (1−11) reflection. Likewise, the lattice distances measured in the electron diffraction pattern in Fig. 2d were d(11–1) = 0.228 ± 0.002 nm, d(−220) = 0.141 ± 0.002 nm, and d(−311) = 0.122 ± 0.002 nm, exhibiting a similar shortening of the (11−1) spacing of about 2% (blue vectors).


Fig. 2 (a and c) TEM images of two single-crystalline isolated TO particles subjected to high pressure. (b and d) Their corresponding electron diffraction patterns show a lattice compression of {111} planes (highlighted in blue color).

The lattice distortion was also observed through aberration-corrected STEM-HAADF imaging. Fig. 3a–h show the atomic-resolution images of two representative particles oriented in the [110] axis zone, as well as the fast Fourier transform (FFT) images in which each indexed plane is shown. The intensity profiles presented in Fig. 3d and h, taken from the magnified areas in Fig. 3c and g, highlight the difference in {111} distances of each particle. Both particles exhibited an asymmetrical distortion, with considerable compression of the (−111) planes, and slight elongation of the (−11–1) planes, generating a ratio between the {111} planes different from the expected d(1–11)/d(−111) = 1. The particle in Fig. 3a shows as well a small contraction along the (−220) plane. The measured difference represents a compression of ∼2.5% and ∼3.4%, respectively, which also generates an angle variation between lattice planes.

(c and g) Magnified areas indicated in (a and e). (d and h) Averaged intensity profile plots of the {111} planes indicated in (c and g) highlighting the lattice distortion.

Multiple particles were analyzed showing variations between 1.8% and 2.8% in the lattice distances. Table 1 summarizes the averaged distances measured from the different lattice planes and the averaged difference percentage. The dMeasured column represents the smaller measured lattice distance of each particle for a specific plane, while the dReference column corresponds to the larger measured distance of the same family planes (for example, for both 111 reflections when a particle is viewed from a 110-axis zone), or to the expected undistorted dhkl. It is worth mentioning that calibration errors were minimized by using a standard Au thin film and the as-synthesized pristine Au nanoparticles as references, and lattice distances in diffraction patterns and FFT images were determined at the center of gravity of the spots.47 This error type cannot produce the observed asymmetric distortion. The excitation error caused by the effect of tilting the specimen out of axis zone generates changes in the intensities of the diffraction spots and could lead to apparent changes in the lattice plane positions. Nevertheless, the expected distance variation, as well as the measured standard error, is below 1%.45 Only those distances exceeding a 1% difference were considered to avoid these geometrical effects. The asymmetrical distortion of the lattice distances, more clearly observed in the {111} planes because the appearance of two-family planes in the projections along the [110] axis zone, suggests a lattice distortion along two axes of the unit cell not observed in the pristine samples (not subjected to high-pressure). An orthorhombic distortion can explain the latter, where lattice parameters are a ≠ b ≠ c, generating splitting of the {111} planes.26

In a DAC with alcohol mixtures as transmission media, the force acting on the sample can be considered as hydrostatic for induced pressures below 11 GPa,17 and in such a case it is expected that a single particle undergoes a uniform compression in all its faces. However, for the used transmission medium at higher pressures the hydrostatic regime is lost, as shown in Fig. 1b. The non-hydrostatic pressure could generate crystallization of the transmission media, and therefore the force acting on the sample would be uniaxially applied. The latter case can lead to plastic deformation or to the generation of defects such as stacking faults or partial dislocations to release the internal stress.

The achieved pressure is considered non-hydrostatic. Furthermore, a geometrical phase analysis (GPA) was performed to detect the possible defects generated on the compressed nanoparticles. The HR-TEM images of an isolated TO particle, as well as the x-component, y-component, and xy-component of the strain field (ε) obtained are shown in Fig. 4. Two {111} reflections were taken for the analysis, in which the measured d spacing difference was around 2.5%. The εxx map showed that strain is accumulated asymmetrically along the particle. More compressive lattice strain was located at one side of the particle, with a highly compressed area near the surface (Fig. 4b). More outer layers were subjected to more tensile strain. Also, a large compressive strain is observed from the εyy map near the (002) top surface. Shear strain was localized near the top {002} facet and right {111} edges of the gold nanoparticle, observing a εxy gradient from one side to the other, suggesting that an asymmetrical distribution of the applied pressure took place during the experiment.

(b–d) Overlay of the HR-TEM image and the calculated strain field (ε) image of the x-component, y-component and xy-component.

Interestingly, measured particles with a triangular shape did not present an observable distortion of their lattice planes. Because of their shape, these particles are commonly found lying with their large {111} facets parallel to the carbon film, and unlike the truncated octahedral particles, these triangular particles contain a twin plane parallel to the large (111) exposed facets. An isolated triangular particle oriented along the [111] direction is presented in Fig. 5. The FFT images with the indexed spots and a magnified area of the particle are also shown, from which the average intensity profiles were acquired from the areas marked in Fig. 5c. The profiles were taken along the <110> directions, and unlike the TO particles, no significant lattice distortion was observed, with d{220} = 0.143 nm and 60° angle between reflections.


Fig. 5 (a) HAADF-STEM high-magnification image of an isolated triangular particle subjected to high-pressure. (b) The corresponding FFT image encompassing the particle area. (c) Magnified area indicated in (a). (d) Averaged intensity profile plots of the {220} planes indicated in (c) showing no significant lattice distortion.

Triangular nanoplates are bound by two {111} planes as the top and bottom faces, separated by a twin boundary plane at the middle of the particle.48–50 The presence of the twin plane along the basal plane of the triangular nanoparticles provided extra stability against the compression by the medium in the DAC. Those particles that did not contain a twin plane underwent a major compression. The presence of twin boundaries, characteristic of small gold particles,51 has been shown to act as barriers for the propagation of dislocations when gold nanoparticles are subjected to external compression.52 Although both particles exhibited exposed {111} facets, the response to the compression was different. This behavior is in agreement with the observed one for hexagonal Pd nanoplates,27 where the multiple stacking faults in the direction perpendicular to the basal plane of the plates led to uniaxial compression along the {111} direction without phase transition.

A larger compression along the [111] direction than in the [200] direction has been observed for Ag and Pd nanoparticles undergoing a phase distortion below 10 GPa of pressure due to a distortion mediated by accumulative strain in a preferential crystallographic direction in single-crystalline particles or grain-boundary mediated distortion for multiple-twinned nanoparticles.26,27 The distortion along two <111> axes observed in our study can be understood from the truncated-octahedral shape of the particles which expose larger {111} facets with smaller {002} facets (Fig. 6), with an average ratio of the surface area between these facets, S111/S002, of ∼12.

Considering that the compression inside the DAC overcame the hydrostatic limit, shear stress can generate a non-homogeneous large force acting along the <111> and <001> directions. These non-hydrostatic conditions could generate plastic deformation in large particles.19 Yang and collaborators reported an overall phase shift evolution as a function of the pressure of large Au nanoparticles (∼400 nm).28 They indicated that at relatively low pressures in a DAC, high-strain areas were located in the corners where two {111} planes and one {100} plane intersect, and increasing the pressure up to 6.4 GPa, a strain redistribution to the particle core took place decreasing the overall strain level. The particle size in our study is in the order of 20 times smaller; hence fast propagation of strain from the particle surface is expected, being limited by the boundary conditions of the finite particle size. The strain is released by dislocation motion to the surface, but if it cannot be released during compression a competition between the strain components from each facet and corner of the particle surface, mediated by shear stress imposed by the experimental conditions, produced an asymmetrical compression. The inhomogeneous compression could have generated a high accumulative strain inducing a change in the atomic arrangement starting at the particle surface, a rearrangement that remained after releasing the pressure and further characterization.

Moreover, no apparent morphology changes were observed in the recovered sample, suggesting the absence of plastic deformation as in the case of large multiple twinned particles. Pseudoplasticity in FCC nanoparticles subjected to compressive stress has been recently observed by Sun24 and Gu,25 where sub-10 nm Au and Ag particles recovered their original shape after releasing the pressure. However, no phase transition was reported. The morphology dependence of deformation mechanisms in small particles has been reported in several studies, where the mechanical response to compressive stress varied along different crystallographic directions depending on the exposed facets and shape.53–57

The strain maps showed the inhomogeneous distribution of strain along the particle which explains the splitting of high-index reflection observed in the electron diffraction patterns. The distorted lattice relaxation to the FCC lattice can be slow under ambient conditions since monitoring of the lattice distortion was conducted several weeks after the compression experiment, or it could be induced by annealing at moderate temperature.58 Our results confirmed that not only size effects play critical roles in the properties of nanomaterials, but also strong surface/morphological effects are observed at the nanoscale.59–62

Finally, the orthorhombic distortion can be calculated from the ideal fcc structure via the ratio of the d spacings of the {200} to the {111} peak. The d111/d200 ratio in bulk gold is 1.15, while in the compressed nanoparticles varied from 1.13 to 1.18, showing the split of two {111} reflections generated by a lattice distortion. Taking as a base the measured d interplanar distances, the distorted parameter, cort, calculated from the average {002} distance would be cort = 0.396 nm ± 0.002 nm, with aort = 0.289 nm ± 0.003 nm and bort = 0.281 nm ± 0.003 nm forming a BCO cell. Considering the bulk cell parameter of a, b, c = 0.408 nm and a parameter d = 0.288 nm from the BCT undistorted subcell (Fig. 6d), this distortion represents a compression of 2.9% and 2.4% along the c and b axes, respectively.

Conclusions

Single-crystal truncated-octahedral gold nanoparticles were subjected to a high-pressure compression of 11.16 GPa in a diamond anvil cell. A shape dependent lattice distortion was observed, since single-crystalline nanoparticles showed a lattice deformation, while particles with single or multiple twinned structure did not present it. An orthorhombic distortion of the lattice was proposed which explained the unpaired deformation of the measured lattice distances from atomic-resolution images and electron diffraction patterns. This lattice distortion represented a phase transition from the face-centered cubic structure to an orthorhombic phase at high pressure, possibly driven by the high applied pressure under non-hydrostatic conditions that can induce shear stress during compression. Previous reports supports the reduction of the transition pressure for non-cubic phase of metal nanoparticles, finding that the internal-stress generated unusual phases at ambient conditions.

TEM characterization conducted in our study is a complementary technique that investigates individual nanoparticles. Although a synchrotron X-ray diffraction study would be proposed to monitor in situ the lattice distortion, it would be misleading when different types of particles are present in the sample. In this regard, electron microscopy represents an excellent technique for the fine structural analysis of single particles making the distinction between their different structures possible. It enabled the observation of an irreversible, or partially irreversible, orthorhombic lattice distortion of small particles. This study contributes to delivering insights into the strong dependence of response of nanosized particles under high-pressure not only on the size but also on the shape and exposed facets.
